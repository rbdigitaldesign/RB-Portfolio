The **Law of Common Region** is a Gestalt grouping principle stating that elements located within a clearly bounded visual region are perceived as belonging to the same group — regardless of their other visual properties.

It was described by psychologist **Stephen Palmer** in 1992, as part of a broader re-examination of Gestalt principles that also yielded the principles of Uniform Connectedness and Synchrony. The common region principle captures the effect of *enclosure*: a visible boundary (border, background, or enclosed shape) creates a perceptual unit from whatever is inside it.

**How it works:**
When a visual boundary is present — a box, a shaded region, a card surface — the brain automatically groups the enclosed elements. This grouping can override other cues: elements inside the boundary will be grouped even if they are less similar to each other than to elements outside the boundary.

This is what makes the *card* pattern so powerful in interface design: a border or surface creates a coherent unit from visually diverse content (text, images, buttons, metadata) that would otherwise require explicit labelling to establish as a group.

**Relationship to other Gestalt principles:**
- **Proximity** — elements that are close together are perceived as grouped (spatial cue)
- **Similarity** — elements that look alike are perceived as grouped (visual cue)
- **Common region** — elements within a shared boundary are perceived as grouped (structural cue)
- **Uniform connectedness** — elements connected by lines or shared surfaces are grouped (relational cue)

Common region is generally a stronger grouping cue than proximity or similarity because it is explicit — the boundary is visible, not inferred.

**Design applications:**

*Cards:* The card is the canonical example. A border or elevated surface groups an image, a heading, a description, and a call-to-action into a single perceptual unit. Users understand the card as an item, not as four separate elements.

*Form sections:* Grouping related form fields (e.g., all address fields) within a visually distinct section signals their relationship. This is often more effective than spacing alone.

*Navigation groupings:* Active states that apply a background fill to a navigation item and its sub-items use common region to communicate that these items belong together.

*Sidebars:* A sidebar with a distinct background colour creates a region that is perceptually separate from the main content — useful for navigation, related content, or supplementary information.

*Tooltips and popovers:* A bordered or shadowed container groups the tooltip's content and communicates that it belongs together as a single explanation unit.

**In learning design:**
Course content that groups an activity prompt, its instructions, a submission link, and a deadline inside a single card communicates their relationship structurally. Students understand what belongs to what activity without relying on headings that say "for the following activity, here are the instructions."

**Practical note:**
Common region effects can be created through borders, background fills, box shadows, or background images. The boundary doesn't need to be heavy — a subtle background tint is often enough. The key is that the region must be perceivably distinct from its surroundings.

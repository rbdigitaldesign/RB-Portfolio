First principles thinking is a mode of reasoning that involves **decomposing a problem to its most fundamental, irreducible truths, and then building your understanding or solution back up from those foundations** — rather than reasoning by analogy from existing solutions or inherited conventions.

The concept comes from Aristotle, who described a "first principle" as "the first basis from which a thing is known" — that which cannot be deduced from anything else. In *Posterior Analytics*, he distinguishes between:
- **Reasoning by analogy** — "This is similar to how X is done, so let's do the same"
- **Reasoning from first principles** — "What are the fundamental constraints here, and what does that actually allow?"

**Why reasoning by analogy is the default:**
Analogy is fast and often good enough. Most of the time, understanding how others have solved similar problems is the most efficient path. We don't need to reinvent the wheel every time we need a wheel.

But analogy has a ceiling. It constrains thinking to the solution space that already exists. If you want to go beyond what's been done before — or if what's been done before is wrong for your context — you need to go deeper.

**The decomposition process:**
1. Identify a belief or assumption ("this course needs to be 12 weeks")
2. Ask: *Is this a physical law, or is it a convention?*
3. If it's a convention: *Why does it exist? What problem was it solving? Does that problem still exist in this context?*
4. Strip it away and ask: *What does the actual goal require?*
5. Rebuild from the foundations

**The physics analogy:**
Physicists work in a world governed by actual laws — conservation of energy, the speed of light, thermodynamic limits. First principles thinking borrows this rigour: identify what is genuinely fixed versus what is merely conventional, and design from the true constraints outward.

**In learning design:**
The lecture is the default unit of university education — inherited from a world before printing, when a scholar reading aloud from a manuscript was genuinely the most efficient way to transfer knowledge to a room full of students. That world no longer exists. The question "why are we still doing lectures?" is a first principles question — and the answer, when you strip away convention, is much narrower than most people assume. Contact hours have genuine purpose; the lecture format is one solution among many.

**In UX design:**
Conventional navigation patterns, page structures, and interaction models exist because they solved problems at a particular moment in a particular context. First principles thinking asks whether those contexts still apply — and opens up the possibility that they don't.

The discipline is in having the honesty to distinguish between constraints that are real and constraints that are imagined.

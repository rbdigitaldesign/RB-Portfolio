Biomimicry is the practice of looking to nature's time-tested patterns and strategies as inspiration for solving human design challenges. Coined and popularised by biologist Janine Benyus in her 1997 book *Biomimicry: Innovation Inspired by Nature*, the idea is deceptively simple: **after 3.8 billion years of evolution, nature has already solved most of the problems we're trying to solve — and the solutions are all around us.**

Unlike "biomorphic" design (making things *look* like nature), biomimicry is about learning from nature's *function* and *principles*:

- Not "let's make this chair look like a tree branch"
- But "let's understand how trees distribute load efficiently and apply that structural principle to the chair"

**Three levels of biomimicry (Benyus):**

1. **Mimicking form** — copying a shape or surface (e.g., sharkskin-textured surfaces to reduce drag)
2. **Mimicking process** — copying a natural process (e.g., using spider silk production principles to create stronger fibres)
3. **Mimicking ecosystem** — modelling the relationships and flows within a system (e.g., industrial symbiosis, where one factory's waste becomes another's raw material)

The deepest level — ecosystem mimicry — is often the most transformative but the hardest to achieve.

**Nature's core design principles:**
Benyus identified several strategies that appear across natural systems:
- **Run on sunlight** — use current solar income, not stored reserves
- **Use only the energy you need** — efficiency, not excess
- **Fit form to function** — shape emerges from purpose
- **Recycle everything** — waste equals food in natural systems
- **Reward cooperation** — mutualism and symbiosis outperform competition in stable environments
- **Rely on diversity** — resilience comes from variety, not monoculture
- **Use locally available materials** — don't ship what you can source nearby
- **Respond to local conditions** — adapt to context rather than imposing a universal solution

**Biomimicry in learning design:**
I've applied this framework to course design directly:
- *Kingfisher beak* → smooth, turbulence-free course entry points that minimise cognitive shock
- *Gecko foot adhesion* → micro-assessments that create cumulative traction through many small contact points
- *Termite mound ventilation* → passive scaffolding — self-regulating course structures that don't require constant instructor intervention
- *Mycelium networks* → connectivist learning that builds resilience through distributed knowledge
- *Whale fin tubercles* → desirable difficulties — productive struggle that creates deeper encoding

The underlying meta-principle is powerful: nature doesn't design for scale or disruption. It designs for *fit* — solutions tightly adapted to their context, efficient over time, resilient through diversity.

Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) is a framework for understanding consumer behaviour and design problems that shifts the unit of analysis from **products and demographics** to **the jobs that people are trying to accomplish** in their lives.

The central idea, articulated by Clayton Christensen: *People don't buy products. They hire them to do a job.*

When someone buys a drill, they don't want a drill — they want a hole. When someone hires a milkshake, they might not want a milkshake — they might want something to do with their hands on a boring commute. Understanding the job reveals design opportunities that product features and demographic segmentation typically miss.

**The milkshake study:**
Christensen and colleagues studied McDonald's milkshake sales to understand why they sold well in the morning. Traditional analysis (demographics, flavour preferences, price sensitivity) wasn't revealing anything useful. When they asked customers to describe what was happening in their life when they bought a milkshake, a pattern emerged: morning milkshakes were being hired to do the job of *commute companion* — something that kept people occupied and mildly satisfied on a long, boring drive. The thick consistency was a feature, not a bug: it lasted the whole commute.

This insight — invisible to feature-level analysis — led to different design decisions: thicker milkshakes, smaller sizes for different commute lengths, faster service. The job was the unit of design, not the product.

**Functional, emotional, and social jobs:**
Most "hires" involve multiple dimensions:
- **Functional job** — the practical task (get a hole in the wall)
- **Emotional job** — how the person wants to feel during or after (confident, capable)
- **Social job** — how they want to be perceived by others (competent homeowner, innovative business)

Often the emotional and social jobs drive behaviour more than the functional one.

**JTBD in learning design:**
Students hire courses for many different jobs simultaneously:
- Get a qualification (credential job)
- Learn a skill (functional learning job)
- Meet people in their field (social and network job)
- Signal competence to an employer (social/career job)
- Structure and accountability for self-improvement (motivation job)

A course designed only for the functional learning job will underserve students for whom the credential or the community is the primary hire. Understanding the full job portfolio shapes what success looks like — and what design decisions actually matter.

**The research method:**
JTBD research focuses on the *moment of hire*: what was happening in the person's life just before they decided to hire this product or course? What were they dissatisfied with? What were they hoping for? This "timeline interview" approach (developed by Bob Moesta) surfaces the context, struggle, and desired outcome that drove the decision — the real job — rather than the post-hoc rationalisation that surveys and focus groups typically produce.

The **Law of Prägnanz** — from the German *Prägnanz*, meaning conciseness or pithiness — is the central principle of Gestalt psychology. It states that the brain will always organise perceptual experience into the simplest, most stable, most regular form available.

It was articulated by **Max Wertheimer, Wolfgang Köhler**, and **Kurt Koffka** in the early twentieth century as the foundational claim of the Gestalt approach: perception is not a passive accumulation of sensory data but an active organisation of experience into coherent, economical wholes.

**What "simplest" means:**
In Gestalt terms, the "simplest" interpretation is the one with:
- The fewest distinct elements
- The most regularity and symmetry
- The most predictable structure
- The highest internal consistency

When an image is ambiguous — when multiple interpretations are possible — the brain will default to the one that requires the least complexity to maintain. This is sometimes called the **minimum principle**: perception seeks minimum complexity.

**Famous demonstrations:**

*The Rubin vase:* The ambiguous figure that can be seen as either two faces or a vase. Most people see one interpretation first (usually the simpler or more familiar), and switching requires deliberate effort.

*Overlapping circles:* Three overlapping circles are seen as three circles, not as a complex compound shape with irregular edges, even though both interpretations fit the same image.

*Logos:* Many famous logos exploit Prägnanz by using incomplete shapes that the brain "completes" into a simpler whole. The WWF panda is constructed from sparse brushstrokes, but the brain perceives a coherent panda. The FedEx logo contains an arrow in the negative space between E and x — perceived when you know to look.

**Design implications:**

*Users impose order:* Even in chaotic or ambiguous designs, users will construct mental models. The question is whether the model they construct matches the one the designer intended. Designing for Prägnanz means making the intended order the obviously simplest interpretation.

*Whitespace and alignment:* A well-aligned, well-spaced layout is easier to process because it provides clean groupings — simple Gestalts. A cluttered, inconsistently spaced layout forces more cognitive work because the Prägnanz is harder to find.

*Iconography:* Simple, regular icon designs are more quickly recognised and remembered than complex, detailed ones. The brain's preference for simple forms means that an icon with five carefully chosen lines communicates more cleanly than a photorealistic rendering.

*Feedback and states:* States that communicate through simple, regular signals (colour changes, clear shape changes) are easier to parse than states that communicate through complex visual patterns.

**In learning design:**
Diagrams, concept maps, and instructional graphics that use regular, aligned layouts leverage Prägnanz — the brain will perceive more structure in a tidy diagram than an equivalent but irregular one. Consistent visual language across a course (same icon set, same colour coding, same layout patterns) creates a visual grammar that becomes a simple, learnable Gestalt.

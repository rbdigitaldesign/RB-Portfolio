John Maeda — computer scientist, graphic designer, and former president of the Rhode Island School of Design — published *The Laws of Simplicity* in 2006. The book is built around ten laws and three keys that explore what simplicity really means in design, technology, business, and life.

**The Ten Laws:**

1. **Reduce** — The simplest way to achieve simplicity is through thoughtful reduction
2. **Organize** — Organization makes a system of many appear fewer
3. **Time** — Savings in time feel like simplicity
4. **Learn** — Knowledge makes everything simpler
5. **Differences** — Simplicity and complexity need each other
6. **Context** — What lies in the periphery of simplicity is definitely not peripheral
7. **Emotion** — More emotions are better than less
8. **Trust** — In simplicity we trust
9. **Failure** — Some things can never be made simple
10. **The One** — Simplicity is about subtracting the obvious and adding the meaningful

**The Three Keys:**
- **Away** — More appears like less by simply moving it far, far away
- **Open** — Openness simplifies complexity
- **Power** — Use less, gain more

**The tension at the heart of simplicity:**
Maeda is careful to distinguish simplicity from minimalism. The goal isn't to remove things arbitrarily — it's to remove things that *obscure meaning or usefulness*, while retaining everything that adds genuine value. Law 5 is particularly important: *simplicity and complexity need each other.* A blank white canvas isn't beautiful — it's boring. A single focal point against a clean background works because of the contrast.

**Reduce as a discipline:**
The first and most important law. Maeda argues that reduction should be *thoughtful* — you need to know what's truly core before you remove anything. The sequence: identify what matters most, then remove everything that isn't that. This requires more effort than adding does, which is why truly simple design is rare.

**Why time is a form of simplicity:**
Maeda's third law is counterintuitive but powerful: making something *faster* makes it feel simpler, even if the underlying complexity is unchanged. This is why a progress indicator reduces perceived waiting time, and why loading screens with meaningful animations feel less frustrating than blank ones. Perceived speed is a design material.

An **affordance** is the relationship between an object (or interface) and a person that signals how the object can or should be used. The term was coined by psychologist J.J. Gibson and adapted for design by Don Norman in his landmark book *The Design of Everyday Things*.

**Gibson's original concept:**
Gibson described affordances as the *action possibilities* that exist in an environment relative to an organism. A branch affords sitting-on for a bird; a flat rock affords sitting-on for a human. Affordances aren't properties of the object alone — they emerge from the relationship between the object and the user.

**Norman's design application:**
Norman brought the concept into design by distinguishing between:

- **Real affordances** — what actions are actually possible with an object
- **Perceived affordances** — what actions a person *believes* are possible based on how the object looks
- **Signifiers** — sensory signals that communicate affordances (Norman later introduced this term to address confusion in how 'affordance' was being used)

For design purposes, **perceived affordances are what matter most.** A glass floor affords walking on it (real affordance) but most people won't walk on it because the perception is fragile (perceived affordance fails). Design is the work of aligning perception with reality.

**The famous door example:**
Norman famously describes doors that have handles on both sides — inviting you to pull — even when you need to push. The handle is a false affordance: it suggests the wrong action. He calls these "Norman doors" (a term coined by others after the book). You've seen them everywhere once you know what to look for.

**False affordances and missing affordances:**
- *False affordance:* design that implies an action that isn't available (a greyed-out button that looks active)
- *Missing affordance:* design that doesn't communicate an available action (a swipeable carousel with no visual indicator that it moves)

**In digital design:**
- Raised, shadowed buttons signal pressability
- Underlined blue text signals a link
- A field with a visible border signals "type here"
- A drag handle (three lines or dots) signals draggability
- Hover states confirm interactivity before commitment

**In learning design:**
An activity with strong affordances makes it immediately clear what kind of response is expected. "Write a 200-word reflection" has strong affordances. "Engage with this content" does not. Good instructional design communicates its intent through structure, not just through instructions.

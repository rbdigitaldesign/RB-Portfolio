Spaced repetition is a learning technique grounded in the science of memory: **reviewing material at strategically spaced intervals produces far better long-term retention than concentrated, massed practice.**

**The forgetting curve:**
In 1885, Hermann Ebbinghaus conducted some of the first rigorous experiments on human memory, documenting what is now called the **forgetting curve**. Without review, we forget roughly:
- 40% of new material within 20 minutes
- 56% within one hour
- 66% within one day
- ~80% within a month

The curve is steep — but it flattens each time we successfully retrieve and review the material.

**The spacing effect:**
The antidote to the forgetting curve is spaced practice: reviewing material just before you would forget it. Each successful retrieval:
1. Resets the forgetting curve (the decay starts over)
2. Flattens it (the next curve is less steep — you forget more slowly)
3. Deepens encoding in long-term memory

The result: material reviewed with optimal spacing is retained far longer, with far less total study time, than the same material reviewed through massed practice (cramming).

**Why it feels harder:**
Robert Bjork's work on desirable difficulties explains a counterintuitive finding: spaced practice *feels* harder than massed practice because you've forgotten more between sessions. The effort of retrieval is uncomfortable. But that effort is precisely what produces durable learning. Cramming feels efficient because material is fresh — but it doesn't survive the week.

**Optimal intervals:**
Spaced repetition systems (SRS) like Anki use algorithms (often based on the SM-2 algorithm developed by Piotr Woźniak) that dynamically calculate the ideal interval for each piece of information based on your history of getting it right or wrong. Easy items get longer intervals; difficult ones get shorter.

Rough starting intervals for new material:
- First review: 1 day after learning
- Second review: 3–5 days
- Third review: 1–2 weeks
- Fourth review: 1 month
- Then: expanding intervals based on accuracy

**In course design:**
Applying spaced repetition to course design means:
- Building retrieval practice into each new week (brief quizzes on prior material)
- Revisiting key concepts at multiple points in a curriculum (spiral design)
- Using low-stakes, frequent assessment rather than single high-stakes exams
- Designing content sequences that naturally resurface earlier ideas in new contexts

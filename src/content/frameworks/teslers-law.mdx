**Tesler's Law** — also known as the **Law of Conservation of Complexity** — states that every application has an inherent amount of complexity that cannot be reduced. It can only be distributed: either the designer/developer absorbs it, or the user does.

The law was formulated by Larry Tesler, a computer scientist at Xerox PARC and Apple (inventor of cut/copy/paste), who observed that no matter how much you simplify a user interface, you never truly remove complexity from the system — you relocate it.

**The implication:**
When a designer makes something "simpler," one of three things is happening:
1. **The designer absorbed the complexity** — building systems, rules, smart defaults, and infrastructure behind the scenes so the user doesn't encounter it
2. **The complexity was pushed onto the user** — hidden menus, unclear defaults, undiscoverable features that the user must eventually discover
3. **The complexity was pushed onto another person** — support staff, system administrators, developers maintaining downstream systems

Good design chooses option 1 deliberately and consistently.

**Where designers go wrong:**
A common mistake is conflating *visual simplicity* with *actual simplicity*. A minimalist interface with three buttons and no labels is visually clean but may be highly complex for the user, who must now discover what those three buttons do through trial, error, or help documentation. The complexity wasn't removed — it was hidden.

**The other direction:**
Tesler also warned against over-simplification. At some point, further efforts to simplify the interface start breaking the underlying model — introducing inconsistencies, edge cases, or behaviours that confuse rather than clarify. The complexity floor is real; trying to go below it breaks things.

**In practice:**

*Forms:* Smart defaults, pre-filled fields, and address auto-completion are all examples of designers absorbing complexity. Every field a user doesn't have to fill in is complexity the system swallowed.

*Onboarding:* A great onboarding experience does significant work behind the scenes — personalising pathways, setting defaults, inferring preferences — so the user simply arrives at a working, relevant environment.

*Automation:* Scheduling tools, email filters, and recommendation algorithms are interfaces to very complex underlying logic. The complexity is real; it's just been moved to where the user doesn't have to manage it manually.

*Learning design:* A course that presents complex theory cleanly has done enormous work — structuring content, selecting examples, scaffolding progressions. The intellectual complexity of the discipline hasn't been removed; it's been translated and made navigable. That translation work is the designer's job.

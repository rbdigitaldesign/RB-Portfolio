The **Pike Effect** — also called **Pike Syndrome** — describes the phenomenon in which past failure conditions a person (or animal) to stop attempting a behaviour, even after the barrier that caused the failure has been removed.

The name comes from a conditioning experiment: a pike (a predatory freshwater fish) is placed in a tank with small prey fish, separated by a transparent glass partition. The pike repeatedly attacks the glass, fails to reach the prey, and eventually stops trying. When the partition is removed, the pike does not resume hunting — it has learned that the prey is unreachable, and that learned belief persists despite the changed reality.

**The mechanism — learned helplessness:**
The Pike Effect is a specific case of **learned helplessness**, a concept formalised by psychologist Martin Seligman in the 1960s through his research on dogs exposed to inescapable shocks. Animals (and humans) who experience repeated failure in the face of an obstacle eventually generalise that failure: *"this type of thing doesn't work for me."* They stop trying — not because they can't, but because past experience has taught them that trying is futile.

The critical point: the learning is *rational* given the original conditions. The failure was real. The glass was there. What makes it a trap is that the belief persists beyond the conditions that created it.

**Why it matters in design and organisations:**

*Users carrying prior experience:* Users arrive at your product with histories. A person who was badly burned by a similar feature in a previous product — a confusing checkout, a data-losing form, a misleading CTA — may avoid that type of interaction entirely, even when your implementation is genuinely different. The barrier is gone; the conditioned avoidance remains.

*Teams and idea suppression:* In organisations, the Pike Effect explains why teams stop generating certain kinds of ideas. Proposals repeatedly rejected by previous leadership create an invisible constraint that persists under new leadership. Nobody makes the case for the approach, not because it's bad, but because past experience says "this doesn't work here." The culture carries the glass even after the wall is gone.

*Institutional inertia:* Processes maintained long after their original rationale has disappeared are often Pike Effect artefacts. "We've always done it this way" frequently traces back to a real constraint — a regulatory requirement that changed, a technical limitation that was resolved, a business condition that no longer applies. The barrier was removed; the behaviour was not updated.

**Design responses:**

*Acknowledge the history:* When redesigning a feature that failed before, explicitly communicating what's changed — rather than silently relaunching — gives conditioned users a reason to update their mental model.

*Early wins:* Onboarding flows that deliver quick, clear value are partly a response to the Pike Effect. Users sceptical from bad prior experiences need evidence that the old barriers are gone before they'll invest effort.

*Psychological safety:* In teams, the Pike Effect is addressed by creating conditions where trying and failing has lower cost — so the learned association between "trying" and "painful failure" is gradually replaced.

**The flip side:**
The Pike Effect is also a reminder that user behaviour in research is historical, not just current. When users say they "never" do something or "always" avoid something, it's worth asking: when did that pattern start, and what originally caused it? The answer may be a constraint that no longer exists.

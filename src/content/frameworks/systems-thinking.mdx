Systems thinking is a way of understanding and analysing problems by looking at **the whole system and the relationships between its components**, rather than isolating individual parts. It recognises that the behaviour of a system emerges from its structure — the feedback loops, delays, stocks, and flows that connect its elements.

**Core concepts (from Donella Meadows):**

**Stocks and flows:**
- *Stocks* are the accumulations in a system — things that can be measured at a point in time (water in a bathtub, knowledge in a community, trust in a relationship)
- *Flows* are the rates of change — things that fill or drain stocks (learning rate, attrition rate, communication frequency)

Understanding a system means understanding what's accumulating and what drives accumulation or depletion.

**Feedback loops:**
Systems are held together by feedback — information about a stock that influences the flow.

- *Balancing loops* (negative feedback): stabilise the system toward a goal. A thermostat is a balancing loop.
- *Reinforcing loops* (positive feedback): amplify change. Compounding interest is a reinforcing loop. So is social proof, viral spread, and learning momentum.

Most complex system behaviour — oscillation, growth, collapse, resistance to change — can be explained by feedback loop structure.

**Delays:**
The time between an action and its visible effect. Delays cause overshooting and oscillation because we adjust based on outdated information. Recognising delays is critical: many well-intentioned interventions fail because people can't see the system's delayed response and give up or overcorrect.

**Leverage points (Meadows' famous hierarchy):**
Meadows identified twelve places to intervene in a system, from least to most effective. The counterintuitive finding: the leverage points we reach for most often (numbers, parameters) are the least powerful. The most powerful leverage points — changing the goals of the system, changing the rules, and most powerfully, changing the paradigm (the shared ideas that create the system) — are the hardest to shift.

**System archetypes:**
Recurring patterns of system behaviour that appear across very different domains:
- *Fixes that fail* — short-term solutions that create long-term problems
- *Shifting the burden* — treating symptoms while the underlying problem grows
- *Tragedy of the commons* — individual rational actions deplete shared resources
- *Limits to growth* — reinforcing loops run into constraints that halt or reverse growth

**In education and design:**
Course development is a complex adaptive system shaped by competing pressures: academic quality, accreditation requirements, market forces, student needs, staff capacity, technology affordances. Optimising one element without understanding the whole often produces unintended consequences elsewhere. The framework I use in my ASCILITE publication explicitly applies this lens: treat course development as complex, not merely complicated.

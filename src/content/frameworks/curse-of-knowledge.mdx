The curse of knowledge is a cognitive bias in which **a person who knows something finds it extremely difficult to imagine what it is like not to know it**. Once knowledge is acquired, it becomes nearly impossible to reconstruct the mental state of someone encountering it for the first time.

The term was coined in a 1989 paper by economists Colin Camerer, George Loewenstein, and Martin Weber, who identified the bias in the context of financial decision-making. It was later popularised in design, communication, and education contexts by Chip and Dan Heath in *Made to Stick* (2007).

**The classic experiment:**
Heath and Heath describe a Stanford study where participants were divided into "tappers" and "listeners." Tappers were asked to tap out a well-known song on a table; listeners had to identify it. Tappers predicted listeners would identify the song about 50% of the time. The actual success rate was 2.5%. Why? Because the tappers could *hear* the song in their heads as they tapped — but listeners just heard a series of disconnected taps. The tappers couldn't un-hear the melody they already knew.

**Why it's pervasive in design:**
Every expert is cursed by their own expertise. They:
- Use jargon that feels neutral but is opaque to beginners
- Skip steps that feel "obvious" — but only because they've internalised them
- Misjudge the difficulty of tasks they mastered long ago
- Underestimate how long it takes to build prerequisite knowledge
- Interpret confused user behaviour as user error rather than design failure

**The antidote: genuine novice observation**
The only reliable cure for the curse of knowledge is to watch real novices engage with your design — not imagined novices, not colleagues who understand the domain, but actual people encountering it fresh. And critically: *observe without intervening*. Every time you want to say "oh, you just need to..." — that's a curse-of-knowledge moment revealing a design problem.

**In learning design specifically:**
Subject matter experts (academics, industry professionals) are among the most cursed collaborators in instructional design. Their deep knowledge makes them invaluable for content accuracy — and makes them structurally incapable of judging what a student needs to understand that content. The learning designer's job is partly to act as a proxy for the beginner, asking "what does someone need to know before they can understand this?"

**The related concept of expert blind spot:**
Nathaniel Gage and colleagues coined the term "expert blind spot" for the specific manifestation in teaching: the failure to recognise that skills and knowledge that have become automatic for an expert need to be made explicit for a novice. Automaticity hides the steps.

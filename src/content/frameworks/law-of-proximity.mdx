The **Law of Proximity** is a Gestalt principle stating that objects close to each other are perceived as belonging to the same group. Spatial distance communicates relationship — things that are near are assumed to be connected; things that are far apart are assumed to be separate.

It was articulated by **Max Wertheimer** in his 1923 paper on perceptual grouping, and it is one of the most fundamental and universally applicable principles in visual design.

**Why it works:**
The brain uses spatial distance as a shorthand for relatedness. In the physical world, objects that are close together are often causally or functionally related — ingredients near each other at a kitchen counter, tools grouped on a workbench. The brain's pattern-recognition system applies this heuristic to visual arrangements automatically, without deliberate thought.

This means that spatial arrangement is always communicating *something*, whether the designer intends it or not. Placing elements near each other makes a statement about their relationship. Placing them far apart makes an equally strong statement.

**The most common violations:**

*Ambiguous form labels:* A label placed halfway between two input fields — equidistant from both — will be visually assigned to neither (or ambiguously to both). Labels should be closer to their own field than to the adjacent one.

*Floating captions:* An image caption separated from its image by excess whitespace becomes visually detached — the reader must consciously reason about which caption belongs to which image, rather than perceiving the relationship directly.

*Inconsistent navigation grouping:* Navigation items that are evenly spaced don't signal that any of them are in a different category. A gap before a 'Sign out' item communicates that it's distinct from the preceding items — a separate group — without a label needed.

**Proximity vs. other grouping cues:**
Proximity is a strong grouping cue, but it can be overridden by other signals. Shared colour, explicit borders, or visual connection lines can group distant elements effectively. Where proximity and another cue conflict, the other cue may win — which is why understanding all Gestalt principles together is more useful than any one in isolation.

**Robin Williams' application:**
Robin Williams (the designer, not the comedian) made proximity one of her four core CRAP design principles (Contrast, Repetition, Alignment, Proximity). Her formulation: *nothing should be placed on the page arbitrarily; every item should have a visual connection with something else on the page.* Elements that belong together should be grouped together; unrelated elements should be visually separated.

**In learning design:**
Instructions for an activity should be immediately adjacent to the activity. Supporting resources should be near the tasks they support. Deadlines should be near the submission links. When spatial arrangement is thoughtless, learners must actively reason about which content belongs to which task — adding cognitive load that competes with the actual learning.

**Practical application:**
When reviewing a layout, test the proximity principle by asking: for every element on the page, what does it appear to be grouped with? Does that grouping match the intended conceptual relationship? If not, adjust spacing first — before adding labels, headers, or dividers — because space communicates relationship directly.

Progressive disclosure is an interaction design and instructional design principle that involves **presenting only the information or options that are necessary for the current context**, and deferring everything else until it's actually needed.

The principle was formalised in interaction design by Larry Constantine and Lucy Lockwood, and has been extensively discussed by Jakob Nielsen in his usability research. At its core, it recognises that:

1. Humans have limited working memory and attention
2. Not all features/information are relevant at all times
3. Presenting everything simultaneously overwhelms users and learners
4. Revealed complexity at the right moment is welcomed; upfront complexity is just confusion

**Three layers of progressive disclosure:**

**Layer 1 — Core:** The primary action or concept. What does this person need to do or understand *right now*? Design for this first.

**Layer 2 — Secondary:** Related options or context that some users may need. Surface on request or after the primary task is underway.

**Layer 3 — Advanced/Optional:** Power features, edge cases, deep theory. Available, but not prominent. For those who seek it out.

**The relationship to learning design:**
In course design, progressive disclosure manifests as sequencing. You don't introduce calculus before algebra. You don't ask learners to evaluate sources before teaching them how to read critically. Good instructional sequencing *is* progressive disclosure — revealing complexity as learners develop the schema to receive it.

This is also why "optional reading" links matter. You can include richer material without overwhelming learners who are already at capacity — just don't make it part of the critical path.

**The discipline it requires:**
Progressive disclosure demands ruthlessness from the designer. You have to make hard decisions about what belongs to the core experience and what doesn't. The temptation to include everything because someone might want it is the enemy of good progressive disclosure design.

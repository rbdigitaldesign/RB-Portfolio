Connectivism was proposed by George Siemens and Stephen Downes in 2004–05 as a learning theory fit for the digital age. Where behaviourism, cognitivism, and constructivism all model learning as something that happens *inside the individual*, connectivism argues that **knowledge also lives in the connections between people, tools, organisations, and information sources** — and learning is the act of navigating, building, and traversing those networks.

**Core principles of connectivism:**

- Learning and knowledge rest in *diversity of opinions*
- Learning is a process of connecting specialised nodes or information sources
- Capacity to know more is more critical than what is currently known
- Nurturing and maintaining connections is necessary for continual learning
- Currency (up-to-date knowledge) is the intent of all connectivist learning activities
- Decision-making is itself a learning process — choosing what to learn and what that learning means in a shifting reality

**Key distinctions from earlier theories:**

Behaviourism focuses on observable behaviour. Cognitivism focuses on internal mental processing. Constructivism focuses on meaning-making through experience. Connectivism argues that all three have become insufficient in an environment where knowledge:
- grows exponentially and becomes outdated quickly
- is often distributed across tools, people, and organisations rather than held by any individual
- requires ongoing curation, not just one-time acquisition

**Chaos theory connection:**
Siemens draws on chaos theory — the idea that meaning exists in patterns across large, complex systems. A learner who can recognise patterns, navigate complexity, and make good decisions about where to direct attention is more equipped than one who has simply memorised content.

**Professional Learning Networks (PLNs)**
One of the most practical expressions of connectivism is the deliberate cultivation of a PLN: the curated collection of people, communities, tools, and sources you engage with to stay current, challenged, and growing in your field. Your PLN is your ongoing learning infrastructure — and building it intentionally is a learnable, designable skill.

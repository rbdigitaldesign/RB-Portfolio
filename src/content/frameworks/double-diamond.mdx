The Double Diamond is a design process framework developed by the UK Design Council in 2004. It visualises the design process as two diamonds placed end to end, each representing a phase of **divergent thinking** (expanding possibilities) followed by **convergent thinking** (narrowing to a decision).

**The four phases:**

**1. Discover (diverge)**
Explore the problem space broadly. Don't assume you know what the problem is — go find out. User research, stakeholder interviews, observation, competitive analysis, secondary research. The goal is to expand your understanding of the context, the users, and the constraints before defining anything.

**2. Define (converge)**
Synthesise your discoveries into a clear, well-framed problem statement. What is the *actual* problem you're designing for? This is the hardest phase: you're making a commitment about what matters most, what to prioritise, and what to leave out. A well-defined problem is often half the solution.

**3. Develop (diverge)**
Generate many possible solutions to the defined problem. Ideation, co-design, prototyping, experimentation. Don't commit to one direction yet — explore broadly. This is where creative confidence is essential: generate ideas without judging them prematurely.

**4. Deliver (converge)**
Test, refine, and deliver the solution. Prototype with users, iterate based on feedback, select the solution that best addresses the defined problem. Build, test, refine, ship.

**The critical insight:**
The framework makes explicit what many teams skip: there are **two** problems to solve, not one.

1. *What is the right problem?* (Diamond 1)
2. *What is the right solution to that problem?* (Diamond 2)

Jumping straight to Diamond 2 — which is what most projects do under time pressure — risks building excellent solutions to the wrong problem. The Discover and Define phases are not overhead. They're the work that makes the development and delivery phases worth doing.

**The 2019 update:**
The Design Council updated the framework in 2019, adding a surrounding "frame" that includes:
- **Leadership & engagement** (the conditions that make design possible)
- **Methods & approaches** (the tools used within each phase)
- **Mindsets** (the attitudes required — curiosity, collaboration, ambiguity tolerance)

This contextualised the diamonds within organisational and cultural realities, acknowledging that process doesn't happen in a vacuum.

Human-Centred Design (HCD) is a design philosophy and process that **places the needs, context, behaviours, and experiences of the people you're designing for at the centre of every decision.** Rather than beginning with technology, business requirements, or designer preference, HCD begins with deep understanding of the human beings who will use the result.

**The three lenses of HCD (IDEO):**
IDEO's framework for HCD organises the process around three overlapping criteria:

1. **Desirability** — What do people actually want and need? (Human lens)
2. **Feasibility** — What is technically possible? (Technology lens)
3. **Viability** — What can be sustained as a business or organisation? (Business lens)

Good design sits at the intersection of all three. HCD insists on starting with desirability — without which feasibility and viability are exercises in building things nobody wants.

**The HCD process:**
The process is typically iterative and non-linear, moving through phases:

**Empathise** — Understand the people you're designing for through observation, interviews, shadowing, and immersion. The goal is to understand not just what people do, but why — their mental models, workarounds, frustrations, and aspirations.

**Define** — Synthesise your research into insights and a clear problem statement. Who is this person, what do they need, and what's getting in the way?

**Ideate** — Generate a wide range of possible solutions before committing to any. Diverge before you converge.

**Prototype** — Build rough, quick representations of your ideas. The goal is to make your thinking tangible so you can test it with real people — not to build something polished.

**Test** — Put your prototype in front of real users and observe how they interact with it. What you learn should directly inform the next iteration.

**The key mindsets:**
- *Empathy over assumption* — what you think users want is less reliable than what you observe them doing
- *Embrace ambiguity* — the early phases of HCD are deliberately open-ended; resisting the urge to jump to solutions too early is a discipline
- *Learn from failure* — prototyping and testing exist to surface failure cheaply, before it's expensive
- *Iterate* — design is never done; it's continuously improved based on evidence from real use

**HCD in learning design (LX design):**
Applying HCD to learning means treating the learner's experience with the same rigour as a product team treats user experience. This includes:
- Learner interviews before course design begins
- Usability testing of course navigation and activity design
- Formative evaluation at multiple points during a course's life
- Designing for the learner's full context: their workload, motivation, prior knowledge, access constraints, and relationship with the institution

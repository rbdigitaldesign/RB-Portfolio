**Uniform connectedness** is a Gestalt grouping principle stating that elements joined by visual connectors — lines, borders, shared backgrounds, or enclosing shapes — are perceived as belonging together, often more powerfully than elements that are merely close together.

It was proposed by Stephen Palmer and Irvin Rock in 1994 as a supplement to the original Gestalt laws, and it captures something the earlier principles missed: that *explicit* visual connection overrides *implicit* spatial cues.

**How it relates to other Gestalt principles:**
The original Gestalt grouping laws — proximity, similarity, continuity — all operate through inference. The mind *infers* a relationship based on spatial or visual patterns. Uniform connectedness is more direct: the elements are literally connected. This makes it a stronger cue.

Experiments have shown that when two elements are enclosed in a shared border, they're perceived as grouped even if they're further apart than ungrouped elements that share the same proximity and colour.

**Visual mechanisms:**
- **Shared borders or outlines** — placing two or more elements inside a box communicates relationship
- **Background fills** — a coloured or shaded region that contains elements unifies them
- **Lines and arrows** — connecting elements with a line creates a direct relational statement
- **Underlines and overlines** — used in navigation to communicate selection or grouping

**Design applications:**

*Cards:* The card UI pattern is a direct application of uniform connectedness. A border or background surface groups content (image, title, description, CTA) as a coherent unit, even though those elements might vary widely in type.

*Forms:* Fieldsets — groups of related form fields visually enclosed — communicate that those fields address the same topic (e.g. "Shipping address"). This reduces cognitive overhead: the user understands the group before reading each field.

*Navigation:* An active tab connected to the content panel below it (through a shared border or the visual "removal" of the separating line) communicates that this tab is the selected one — a structural affordance rather than a purely stylistic one.

*Dashboards:* Panels that group related metrics help users understand at a glance which data belongs together, without requiring headings to do all the work.

**In learning design:**
When a course activity and its instructions share a visual container — a card, a shaded box, a bordered panel — students understand they belong together without having to read a heading that says "Instructions for this activity." The visual structure communicates the conceptual relationship.

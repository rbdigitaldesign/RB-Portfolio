Cognitive Load Theory (CLT) was developed by educational psychologist John Sweller in 1988. It's grounded in one core biological reality: **working memory is severely limited**. We can only hold and process about four chunks of new information at a time before things start to fall apart.

Sweller identified three types of cognitive load:

- **Intrinsic load** — the inherent complexity of the material itself. You can't change the fact that calculus is harder than addition. But you can sequence content to build the schema a learner needs before introducing complexity.

- **Extraneous load** — the cognitive effort caused by *poor design*. Unnecessary jargon, cluttered interfaces, redundant instructions, unclear navigation — all of this burns cognitive resources without contributing to learning or task completion. This is the designer's responsibility to eliminate.

- **Germane load** — the productive cognitive effort that actually leads to learning: making connections, building mental models, applying knowledge in new contexts. This is what we want. The goal of good design is to free up as much mental bandwidth as possible *for this*.

The theory has massive implications for both instructional and interface design. If a learner or user is working hard just to *parse* what's in front of them, they have nothing left for the actual task. Complexity that isn't inherent to the subject matter is a design failure — not a user failure.

**Key techniques that reduce extraneous load:**
- The *worked example effect*: show a complete solution first, then gradually fade guidance
- The *split-attention effect*: integrate text and diagrams rather than separating them (so the eye doesn't have to travel back and forth)
- The *redundancy effect*: don't present the same information in two formats simultaneously (e.g. reading aloud text that's also on screen)
- Chunking: break content into meaningful, digestible units
- Sequencing: order content to build prerequisite knowledge before introducing complexity

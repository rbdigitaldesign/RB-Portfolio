Hick's Law (also called the Hick-Hyman Law) states that **the time it takes to make a decision increases logarithmically with the number and complexity of choices available.**

It was named after British psychologists William Edmund Hick and Ray Hyman, who independently published research in 1952 demonstrating this relationship. Hick measured the response time of participants as the number of possible stimuli increased. The relationship is:

**RT = b × log₂(n + 1)**

Where RT is reaction time, b is a constant, and n is the number of choices. The logarithmic relationship means that doubling choices doesn't double decision time — but it does meaningfully increase it, especially as choice sets grow large.

**Why it matters beyond reaction time:**
The law's practical implications go beyond the milliseconds of reaction time Hick was measuring. In real-world design:
- **More choices increase cognitive load** — evaluating options requires working memory
- **More choices increase decisional anxiety** — the fear of choosing wrong grows with the stakes and the set size
- **More choices can paralyse** — the phenomenon Barry Schwartz called the "paradox of choice": beyond a certain point, more options lead to *less* choosing, not more

**The jam study:**
Psychologists Sheena Iyengar and Mark Lepper conducted a famous field experiment in 2000: a gourmet food store set up a display table featuring either 24 varieties of jam or 6. While the 24-jam display attracted more initial interest, the 6-jam display resulted in ten times more purchases. Choice overload had converted interest into paralysis.

**Design applications:**

*Navigation:* Limit primary navigation items to 5–7. Use information architecture to categorise, not just list.

*Forms:* Group related fields, use smart defaults to reduce the number of active choices, and use progressive disclosure to hide fields that aren't relevant yet.

*Feature sets:* Curate rather than accumulate. Every additional feature is an additional choice the user must process and evaluate, even passively.

*Learning design:* Too many optional resources, too many assignment formats, too many pathways — these don't feel like freedom; they feel like burden. Constraint is a gift to the learner.

**The relationship to cognitive load:**
Hick's Law is essentially a specific expression of cognitive load theory in the context of choice. The extraneous load of evaluating a large choice set competes with the cognitive resources available for the actual task. Reduce the choice set, reduce the load, free up capacity.

**The counterargument:**
Hick's Law doesn't mean fewer choices are always better. Offering one choice is a monopoly, not a design. The principle is about *the right number of choices, well-organised* — not the minimum possible. Categorisation is powerful: 20 well-organised options can feel easier than 6 poorly labelled ones.

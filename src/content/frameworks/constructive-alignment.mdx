Constructive alignment is a principle of course design developed by John Biggs in 1996. It holds that **learning outcomes, assessment tasks, and teaching/learning activities should all be explicitly aligned** — pointing toward the same intended capability or understanding.

The word "constructive" carries a double meaning:
1. It refers to **constructivism** — the idea that learners actively construct knowledge rather than passively receive it
2. It refers to the **construction of the learning environment** to support that knowledge-building

**The three elements:**

**1. Intended Learning Outcomes (ILOs)**
Clear, specific statements of what learners will be able to *do* (not just know) at the end of the learning experience. Written with Bloom's taxonomy-level verbs: *analyse*, *evaluate*, *create*, *apply*, *explain*, *identify*.

The specificity matters enormously. "Understand X" is not a measurable outcome. "Evaluate X using criteria Y and Z" is.

**2. Assessment tasks**
What students actually *do* to demonstrate their learning. For constructive alignment to hold, every assessment must directly measure the stated learning outcomes — not proxy skills, not effort, not attendance.

The assessment is the primary driver of student behaviour. Students ask "what's on the exam?" — rightly. If the assessment doesn't match the outcomes, students who focus on the assessment are being punished.

**3. Teaching and learning activities**
The weekly work — lectures, discussions, readings, problem sets, projects. These should provide learners with the practice, knowledge, and feedback they need to succeed at the assessments.

**Why misalignment is so common:**
- Outcomes are often written at the start of curriculum development and forgotten by the time assessments are designed
- Assessment tasks are often inherited from previous course iterations
- Teaching activities are often content-driven ("what do I need to cover?") rather than outcome-driven ("what do students need to be able to do?")
- Competing pressures (content coverage, marking loads, academic traditions) pull these elements in different directions

**The SOLO taxonomy:**
Biggs also developed the SOLO taxonomy (Structure of Observed Learning Outcomes) as a tool for describing the quality of learning responses:
1. *Pre-structural* — misses the point
2. *Uni-structural* — identifies one relevant aspect
3. *Multi-structural* — identifies several aspects but doesn't connect them
4. *Relational* — integrates aspects into a coherent whole
5. *Extended abstract* — generalises beyond the task to new domains

SOLO is useful both for writing assessment rubrics and for setting outcome targets: "students will demonstrate *relational* understanding of X" is a more precise ambition than "students will understand X."

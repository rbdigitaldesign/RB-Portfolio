The **Pareto Principle** — also called the **80/20 rule** — states that, in many systems, roughly 80% of the effects come from 20% of the causes.

It was named after Italian economist **Vilfredo Pareto**, who observed in 1896 that approximately 80% of Italy's land was owned by 20% of the population. He also noted the same distribution in his garden: 20% of the peapods contained 80% of the peas. Management consultant Joseph Juran generalised this observation into a principle for quality management, calling it the "vital few and trivial many."

**What it is and isn't:**
The 80/20 split is not a mathematical law — it's an empirical observation about how unevenly effects tend to be distributed. Real-world distributions vary: it might be 70/30 or 90/10. The principle's value is not in the specific ratio but in the *mindset it promotes*: look for disproportionate contributors and concentrate effort there.

**Why distributions are uneven:**
Many real-world systems exhibit power law distributions rather than normal distributions. In these systems, a small number of inputs have outsized leverage — whether through network effects, compounding, skill concentration, or the structural advantages of early leaders. The Pareto Principle is a heuristic for navigating this kind of system.

**Design applications:**

*Feature prioritisation:* User analytics almost always show that a minority of features account for the majority of usage. Identifying and optimising that vital 20% — rather than spreading effort evenly across all features — produces better return on design effort.

*Bug fixing:* In software quality management, a small number of bugs typically account for the majority of user-facing failures. Pareto analysis (charting defects by frequency) identifies the highest-leverage fixes.

*Performance optimisation:* Web performance is often dominated by a small number of bottlenecks. Identifying and resolving the top few usually produces far more improvement than incremental work across the board.

*Content strategy:* Traffic and engagement on most websites is heavily concentrated in a small number of pages. Understanding which pages drive most value (and why) informs where to focus content investment.

**In learning design:**
A course can be analysed through a Pareto lens: which 20% of the learning activities produce the most measurable progress toward the stated learning outcomes? Which concepts, when understood, unlock understanding of the others? Start there. Build depth on the vital core before expanding to the periphery.

This principle also informs assessment design: assess the vital concepts deeply rather than sampling everything shallowly.

**The limits:**
The Pareto Principle can be misused to justify neglecting the "trivial many" — but sometimes the 80% matters. Edge cases in software can affect minority users disproportionately. The long tail of content may serve diverse needs that the vital 20% doesn't address. Use it as a prioritisation tool, not a permission to ignore the rest.

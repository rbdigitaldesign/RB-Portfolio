**Parkinson's Law** states that *work expands to fill the time available for its completion.* If you allocate six weeks to a task that could be done in two, it will take six weeks — not because the work is harder, but because the absence of time pressure removes the force that drives prioritisation and decision-making.

The law was articulated by **Cyril Northcote Parkinson** in a 1955 essay published in *The Economist*, written as a satirical analysis of British bureaucracy. Parkinson observed that government departments grew in staff and activity regardless of the actual workload — work expanded to fill the available resources. The principle was later generalised beyond bureaucracy into personal productivity, project management, and design practice.

**Why work expands:**
Several mechanisms drive expansion:

*Perfectionism:* With ample time, there is always something more to refine. Time pressure forces an acceptance of "good enough" that unconstrained time never enforces.

*Scope creep:* When timelines are long, requirements accumulate. New ideas, stakeholder requests, and discovered edge cases attach themselves to the deliverable. A tight timeline creates natural resistance to scope additions.

*Delayed starting:* Generous deadlines enable delay. The actual work is compressed into the final portion of the available time — often worse than if it had been done with a tighter initial deadline.

*Decision avoidance:* Difficult design decisions are easier to defer when time is plentiful. Constraint forces resolution.

**Design and project management applications:**

*Design sprints:* The Google Design Sprint methodology is a direct application of Parkinson's Law. By constraining an end-to-end design process to five days, it forces prioritisation, rapid prototyping, and decision-making that would otherwise be deferred indefinitely.

*Agile sprints:* Two-week sprint cycles create a recurring compression that prevents work from expanding indefinitely. Commitments made at the start of a sprint must be delivered by its end.

*Time-boxed meetings:* Meetings without hard end times expand. Meetings with fixed durations and agendas force prioritisation of what must be discussed versus what would merely be convenient.

*Feedback rounds:* In content production, unlimited revision cycles enable endless refinement. A fixed number of rounds with clear scope constraints forces reviewers to identify what matters most.

**The inverse application:**
Parkinson's Law can be applied deliberately as a productivity tool: set artificially tight deadlines for tasks to compress the work into a smaller window. This is the mechanism behind the Pomodoro Technique (25-minute timed work intervals) — the time limit forces focus in a way that open-ended working time does not.

**In learning design:**
Assignment deadlines are not just administrative formality — they are the primary mechanism that structures student effort. Without deadlines, most students would compress all work into the final days before the course ends. Staggered deadlines across a course distribute effort and prevent Parkinson's Law from generating a last-minute crisis.

**The limits:**
Some work genuinely requires time: deep thinking, synthesis, research, creative incubation. Aggressive time constraints on these tasks can produce shallow, rushed output. Parkinson's Law is most useful as a corrective to *excessive* timelines, not as an argument for minimum possible timelines.

**Fitts's Law** is a predictive model of human motor performance stating that the time required to rapidly move to and select a target is a function of the target's **size** and **distance** from the starting point.

It was formulated by psychologist **Paul Fitts** in 1954 through research on manual aiming tasks. The law is expressed as:

**T = a + b × log₂(2D/W)**

Where T is movement time, D is the distance to the target, W is the width of the target, a and b are empirically derived constants, and the log term is the **index of difficulty** — the harder the task, the higher this value.

The critical insight: **doubling the target size reduces difficulty more than halving the distance.** Size and distance both matter, but size has particular leverage in constrained pointing environments.

**Application to screen design:**

*Button size:* Primary action buttons should be large enough to activate quickly and accurately. A tiny "Submit" button creates unnecessary pointing difficulty. Platform guidelines formalise this: Apple recommends a minimum touch target of 44×44 points; Google's Material Design specifies 48×48 density-independent pixels.

*Position matters:* The further a target is from where the user's cursor or finger currently sits, the longer it takes to reach. This is why frequently-used actions should be close to common starting positions — near the content being acted on, near previous actions, or in screen-edge positions.

*Screen edges and corners:* Screen edges are special in Fitts's Law terms — they act as infinite walls, meaning a cursor moving toward a screen edge will always hit it. This is why Mac OS's menu bar (at the top edge) allows extremely fast access — users can slam the cursor toward the top at full speed and it will land on the menu. Screen corners are doubly powerful (two walls).

*Touch vs. mouse:* A mouse pointer is precise; a finger is not. Fitts's Law is more consequential in touch interfaces because W (target width) must compensate for larger pointing uncertainty. Small touch targets are not just inconvenient — they're physically difficult to activate accurately.

*Dangerous actions:* Fitts's Law can be used deliberately to make certain actions *harder* to activate. Destructive or irreversible actions (delete, log out, cancel subscription) can be made small and distant from primary actions to reduce the likelihood of accidental activation.

**The infinite edge principle in design systems:**
Sticky toolbars, floating action buttons, and context menus that appear at pointer position are all applications of Fitts's Law — reducing movement distance to minimise the time and effort cost of reaching important controls.

**In practice:**
Fitts's Law violations are often identifiable by asking: *what does a user have to do to complete the most common task?* If the answer involves a tiny target, a long pointer journey, or a small touch target at an awkward position, you have a Fitts's Law problem — measurable, predictable, and fixable.

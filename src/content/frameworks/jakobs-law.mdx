**Jakob's Law** states that users spend most of their time on interfaces *other than yours* — and therefore arrive at your product with mental models built from those experiences. Designs that match those existing mental models reduce learning cost and feel intuitive; designs that violate them create friction, confusion, and abandonment.

The law was articulated by **Jakob Nielsen**, co-founder of the Nielsen Norman Group, based on decades of usability research. It formalises a simple but important insight about how human learning and pattern recognition work.

**Mental models:**
A mental model is a person's internal representation of how something works — what actions are possible, what the outcomes of those actions will be, and where things are located. These models are built through experience: every interface a person uses adds to their model of what "an interface" looks like and how it behaves.

When you encounter a new interface that matches your existing mental model, it feels intuitive. When it violates your model, you have to consciously reason about what to do — which is slower, more error-prone, and less satisfying.

**The cost of innovation:**
Novel UI patterns have a real cost: users must unlearn their existing model and construct a new one for your specific product. This isn't impossible, but it requires effort — and users may not be willing to invest that effort if they can't see the payoff immediately.

Jakob's Law doesn't argue against innovation; it argues that **innovation requires justification.** If your new pattern is genuinely better, users will eventually learn it. But "different" is not the same as "better," and different is costly.

**Where conventions come from:**
Design conventions are not arbitrary — they emerge from patterns that proved effective across many products and users over time. The shopping cart icon, the top-right user menu, the bottom navigation bar on mobile, the hamburger icon for a hidden drawer — these exist because they've been broadly adopted and taught to hundreds of millions of users. Deviating from them requires a compelling reason.

**The alternative — progressive innovation:**
When breaking convention is necessary, the best approach is to minimise the deviation and support the transition. Provide clear affordances for the new pattern. Use familiar language even if the visual is new. Include tutorials, tooltips, or onboarding flows that explicitly bridge the gap between expectation and reality.

**In learning design:**
Students arrive in any course with mental models of what "a course" is, what an "assignment" requires, what a "discussion forum" is for — built from every previous learning experience. Deviating sharply from those conventions (unusual submission formats, non-standard naming, novel navigation patterns) creates cognitive overhead that competes with the actual learning. Working within familiar structural conventions frees up cognitive resources for the content itself.

**The limits:**
Jakob's Law can be misused to resist all design improvement. Sometimes conventions are wrong — the skeuomorphic design era is full of conventions that were eventually improved by flat design. The principle doesn't mean never change; it means change with awareness of the unlearning cost, and only when the benefit is proportionate.

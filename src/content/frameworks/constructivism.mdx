Constructivism is one of the foundational theories of how human beings learn. At its core, it holds that **knowledge is not transferred from teacher to learner — it is actively constructed by the learner** through experience, reflection, and engagement with their environment.

There are two major branches:

**Cognitive constructivism (Jean Piaget)**
Piaget argued that learners build individual mental models — *schemas* — based on their experiences. When they encounter new information, they either:
- *Assimilate* it (fit it into an existing schema), or
- *Accommodate* it (revise or create a new schema to account for the new experience)

This is why you can't simply tell someone something and expect understanding to follow. The learner has to connect it to something they already know.

**Social constructivism (Lev Vygotsky)**
Vygotsky emphasised that learning is fundamentally social and cultural. We make meaning *with* others — through dialogue, collaboration, modelling, and shared activity. His concept of the **Zone of Proximal Development** (ZPD) — the gap between what a learner can do independently and what they can do with support — shaped everything from scaffolded instruction to peer learning and mentorship.

**What this means for design:**
- Content delivery alone is not instruction. Activities, discussions, and application are essential.
- Prior knowledge matters enormously — good design activates what learners already know before introducing new material.
- Context is not optional. Learning in authentic, meaningful contexts produces better transfer than abstract, decontextualised content.
- The teacher or designer's role shifts from "expert dispensing knowledge" to "designer of conditions for learning."
- Collaboration isn't a nice-to-have — for social constructivists, it's where much of the real learning lives.

Constructivism sits behind many modern instructional approaches: problem-based learning, project-based learning, inquiry learning, experiential learning, and communities of practice all draw heavily from constructivist principles.

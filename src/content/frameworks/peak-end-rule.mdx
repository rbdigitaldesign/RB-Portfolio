The **Peak-End Rule** is a cognitive heuristic that describes how people evaluate past experiences: not by calculating the average of all moments, but primarily based on two points — **the peak** (the most intense moment, positive or negative) and **the end** (how it felt when it concluded).

The rule was identified by psychologist **Daniel Kahneman** and colleagues, with foundational research by Barbara Frederickson and Daniel Kahneman published in 1993. It is discussed extensively in Kahneman's 2011 book *Thinking, Fast and Slow*.

**The colonoscopy study:**
Kahneman's team studied patients undergoing colonoscopies — an inherently unpleasant procedure. One group received the standard procedure; the other had the procedure extended by a period of less intense (though still uncomfortable) activity at the end. Objectively, the second group had a longer, more painful experience. But when evaluated retrospectively, they rated it as *less* unpleasant — because the ending was less intense. The peak-end average of the second group was more favourable, even though total discomfort was greater.

**What this reveals:**
The "experiencing self" (which lives through each moment in real time) and the "remembering self" (which evaluates experiences retrospectively) do not agree. Memory does not sum up moments — it samples them, weighting peaks and endings disproportionately. The duration of the experience has surprisingly little effect on retrospective evaluation (Kahneman called this *duration neglect*).

**Design implications:**

*End states:* The ending of any experience has disproportionate influence on how it's remembered. A great order confirmation screen, a warm farewell email, a satisfying "you're all done" state — these are investments in retrospective experience quality, recovering goodwill that friction elsewhere may have cost.

*Peaks:* Designing a memorable positive peak — a moment of delight, surprise, or achievement — can elevate the entire retrospective evaluation of an experience. Confetti on task completion, an unexpected personalised detail, a genuine 'wow' moment in an onboarding flow.

*Pain management:* Unavoidable friction (long forms, complex processes) is less damaging when it doesn't constitute the peak or end of the experience. Placing friction in the middle, and delivering value at the end, shifts the retrospective evaluation.

*Customer service:* The resolution and closing of a support interaction are more influential on customer satisfaction scores than the hold time or the initial difficulty. Ending well matters more than eliminating all friction.

**In learning design:**
The final experience of a course — the capstone project, the last synchronous session, the closing reflection — has disproportionate influence on how students remember the entire learning experience. Designing a genuinely excellent ending is one of the highest-leverage investments in overall course quality.

Similarly, a standout lecture, a transformative workshop activity, or a memorable case study becomes the "peak" against which the whole course is evaluated. Deliberately creating these peak moments — rather than aiming for uniform adequacy — is a strategy informed directly by this principle.

**Relationship to other concepts:**
- **Serial Position Effect** — the first and last items in a sequence are best recalled; the Peak-End Rule is the emotional/evaluative version of this
- **Kahneman's two-self model** — the experiencing self vs. the remembering self
- **Experience design** — the field most directly concerned with engineering moments

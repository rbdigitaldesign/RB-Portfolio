The **Zeigarnik Effect** is the psychological tendency to remember uncompleted or interrupted tasks more vividly than completed ones, and to experience a persistent cognitive tension toward their resolution.

It was documented by Soviet psychologist **Bluma Zeigarnik** in 1927. The inspiration came from her supervisor Kurt Lewin, who noticed that waiters in a Viennese café could easily recall unpaid orders but quickly forgot those that had been settled. Zeigarnik formalised this in experiments: participants interrupted mid-task recalled the incomplete tasks roughly twice as often as completed ones.

**Why it happens:**
When a task is initiated, the brain creates an active goal representation — a kind of mental placeholder that persists until the goal is fulfilled. Lewin called this **psychic tension**: an unresolved goal maintains a state of low-level activation, keeping the task accessible in memory and generating a pull toward completion.

When the task is completed, the representation is released, and the cognitive tension resolves. The task is "closed" and moves toward forgetting.

**Design applications:**

*Progress indicators:* Progress bars work partly through the Zeigarnik Effect. Showing a user they are 70% through a process creates unfinished business — the open loop generates motivation to reach 100%. This is why partial progress is more motivating than a blank start.

*Onboarding checklists:* Profile completion indicators ("Your profile is 60% complete") use the effect to motivate users to fill in remaining fields. The incompleteness is made visible, the tension is real.

*Streaks:* Apps like Duolingo and productivity tools use streaks to make an "unbroken chain" feel like an open task. Breaking the streak resolves the tension in the wrong direction — the negative version of the effect.

*Shopping carts:* "You have items in your cart" reminders are direct exploitations of the Zeigarnik Effect — keeping an uncompleted transaction in working attention.

*Content:* Cliffhangers in serialised media are perhaps the oldest use of the effect. The story remains "open" in the reader's mind until the next instalment.

**In learning design:**
A module left 80% complete creates cognitive drag. Course platforms that make incompleteness visible (greyed-out lessons, progress rings, "last visited" markers) leverage the Zeigarnik Effect to motivate return. But this is a two-edged tool: if a learner has too many open loops simultaneously, the effect produces anxiety rather than motivation.

The design implication: **open loops intentionally, and close them clearly.** Each lesson should have a clear completion state. Each activity should signal when it's done. Ambiguity about whether a task is "finished" is cognitive overhead.

**Relationship to other concepts:**
- **Goal-gradient effect** — motivation increases as you approach task completion (a complementary finding)
- **Cognitive closure** — the psychological need to reach a definite answer or conclusion
- **GTD (Getting Things Done)** — David Allen's productivity system is partly an engineered response to Zeigarnik: capture open loops externally so your brain can release the tension

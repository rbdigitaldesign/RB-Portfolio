Poka-yoke (ポカヨケ) is a Japanese term coined by industrial engineer Shigeo Shingo, roughly translated as "mistake-proofing" or "inadvertent error prevention." The idea is to design systems, products, or processes so that errors are physically impossible — or at least immediately obvious and easily correctable.

Rather than training people to be more careful, poka-yoke changes the environment so that the wrong action simply cannot be performed, or triggers an immediate warning when it is.

There are two types:

- **Prevention** (control poka-yoke): Makes the mistake impossible to perform in the first place.
- **Detection** (warning poka-yoke): Allows the mistake to occur but immediately flags it so it can be corrected.

The principle emerged from manufacturing, but it's equally powerful in digital design, architecture, policy, and everyday product design. Whenever you see a design that guides the user toward the correct action through its shape, flow, or constraints — that's poka-yoke in action.

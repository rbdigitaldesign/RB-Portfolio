"Don't Make Me Think" is both a design principle and a 2000 book by usability consultant Steve Krug. The principle is simple: **when a person encounters something you've designed, they should be able to understand it — what it is, what it does, how to use it — without effort, deliberation, or instruction.**

Krug observed that people don't read web pages — they scan. They don't make optimal choices — they satisfice (pick the first reasonable option). They don't figure out how things work — they muddle through, blaming themselves when they can't. Good design accommodates this reality rather than fighting it.

**The self-evident interface:**
Krug argues that good design is *self-evident* — the meaning and function are clear without explanation. The question he proposes every designer ask about every element: *What does this person need to understand, and is the design making that obvious?*

If you find yourself reaching for a tooltip, a help text paragraph, or a tutorial just to explain a basic feature — that's a sign the design needs rethinking, not supplementing.

**Street signs, not puzzles:**
Krug uses the metaphor of street signs vs puzzles. A street sign is designed to be read at speed, from a distance, by a stranger who's never seen it before. It doesn't require context or effort. Most interfaces should work the same way.

**Key heuristics from the book:**
- Create a clear visual hierarchy that matches the importance of things
- Use conventions — familiar patterns users already know
- Break pages into clearly defined areas so people know where to focus
- Make it obvious what's clickable
- Eliminate noise — anything that competes for attention without adding value
- Assume zero reading — can someone understand the page without reading most of it?

**Application beyond interfaces:**
The "don't make me think" principle extends to any designed experience. A course module that requires learners to figure out where to start, what to do next, or whether they're on track is violating this principle just as surely as a confusing website. Clarity of structure and navigation is a form of respect for the person's time and attention.
